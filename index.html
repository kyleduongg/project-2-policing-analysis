Project 2 Are Police Officers the Most Problematic Rank?

Kyle Duong, Scott Huang, & Zihan Zhang — kyduong@ucsd.edu, sch005@ucsd.edu, & zaz003@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Police officers are the most problematic rank.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Decision 1: Isolating Police Officers with a single contrasting color (Score: +1.5, Earnest)
    • The use of the color red for the focal point was done intentionally to contrast the ranks. Red was used to direct attention towards the Police Officer rank, while the other ranks are kept a uniform color (blue); This visually separates the Police Officer rank, which pops out pre-attentively to those who view the graph, from the rest of the ranks through the Gestalt Principles of Similarity. This approach was chosen because it allows people who view this graph to be instinctively directed without making the other bars look split apart. This is earnest because it directs attention without distorting or hiding any data from the audience. An alternative considered prior was using gray instead of blue for the non-target ranks to create a stronger contrast, but gray felt too dull and risked making the ranks seem unimportant to the visualization. Instead, blue was chosen because it provides a smooth contrast with red while keeping the other ranks visually present. The color-coding method is effective because it allows the center of attention to be the rank that is of a different color, however as mentioned before, having only one rank stand out visually would pose a potential risk of having too much contrast where the Police Officer rank is overemphasized, possibly drawing attention away from meaningful comparisons among other ranks.
  • Decision 2: Keeping the Y-Axis at zero (Score: +2, Earnest)
    • The y-axis begins at 0 (0.0) rather than a higher value. This was an important decision that we made because the substantiation rate is a ratio measurement, meaning that it has a zero baseline. When it is 0%, this means no complaints were substantiated for that rank. Starting at zero allowed the length of each bar to be proportional to its actual value. If we didn’t do this, the 7% gap between the Police Officer rank and the next highest would appear dramatically larger, as it would exaggerate this difference even though the difference is only 7%. Starting at 0 is effective because it allows us to represent the true magnitude of the gap rather than trying to deceive someone with the use of a truncated y-axis. However, beginning at 0 also has some limitations because it can make the difference that we want less visually dramatic, reducing the impact that we want to present. The decision is fully earnest because it shows a truthful representation of the findings instead of exaggerating the gaps in data. An alternative was to consider starting the y-axis at a higher value to make the gap seem larger and more dramatic, which would have strengthened the argument. However, this was decided against because it would have been deceptive, and even with a truthful representation, people can still paint the same picture that police officers are indeed the most problematic rank.
  • Decision 3: Calculating the y-axis as substantiation rate per rank (Score -1.5, Deceptive)
    • Police officers hold the largest multitude of personnel, while there are fewer captains and detectives in their respective fields. By default, it would appear that police officers receive the most complaints and therefore have the highest substantiated complaint rate. If we were to present a different metric, such as “substantiated complaints per officer in each rank,” comparison may appear less extreme because this new metric takes into account the number of police officers there are in relation to the rate of complaints. However, by using “substantiation rate per rank” (substantiated complaints divided by total complaints), our focus shifts to how often accusations are confirmed, not how much misconduct each officer contributes. This ignores differences in rank size, which can mislead readers into thinking police officers are the most problematic, despite the metric not accounting for how many people are in each rank. This method works well because it creates a simple, compelling comparison across ranks, making it easy for viewers to identify “the most problematic rank.” However, some people could argue that the metrics are misleading, as it ignores the independent size of each rank.
  • Decision 4: Using bar labels to display exact percentages (Score +2, Earnest)
    • Adding explicit percentage labels on top of each bar was important because our group wanted viewers to see the takeaway as soon as possible, instead of spending their time trying to identify 8 different values. This helps deal with cognitive load and makes the comparison between ranks immediately obvious. In our visualization, viewers can identify that Police Officers are at exactly 30.4% substantiation rate, while the next rank is at 23.4%, without ever having to estimate using the y-axis. This allows them to easily conclude that police officers have the highest substantiation rate among all ranks. This worked well because viewers can identify the exact values and see the gap between Police Officers and other ranks without any ambiguity. This is earnest because it helps to prioritize clarity in our visualization, as we aren’t forcing viewers to take their time to estimate values from bar lengths. An alternative that we fully considered was relying solely on the y-axis grid line, but that would still require more effort from viewers and introduce estimation error. With the addition of keeping these bar labels, viewers can get the precise value and make their conclusion without having to guess what the actual values are.
  • Decision 5: Removing tick marks, top, and right spines to improve data-ink ratio (Score: +1, Earnest)
    • Our group decided to remove the tick marks as well as the top and right spines from the bar chart. This was done to improve the data-ink ratio because we decided to remove any non-essential elements that don’t convey the data or the main takeaway. As a group, we felt that the top and right spines served no purpose other than enclosing the chart, so deleting them reduced any visual clutter and allowed viewers to focus on the bars themselves. The tick marks were a small detail that we removed because they provided no meaningful value to the viewer. Viewers can still interpret the bar heights using the grid lines or the explicit bar labels that we had added. This worked well because the chart looks cleaner and is not cluttered together. Without the extra lines that are competing for attention, we hope that the viewer’s eyes naturally go to the bars and their values. A thing that didn’t work as well is that some viewers are accustomed to a traditional chart style, as they might find the missing spines odd. However, we felt that the trade-off was worth it for the improved clarity. An alternative that we considered was keeping all four of the spines but making them lighter in color. As a group, we decided against this because removing them entirely was more effective at reducing ink. This decision is earnest because we removed ink that doesn’t add any meaning to the visualization.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Decision 1: Normalizing the y-axis by calculating complaints per officer (Score: +1, Earnest)
    • Police officers have substantially more individuals than any other rank. Consequently, it would seem they have the most complaints out of the entire police force when we categorize substantiated complaints by rank alone. Therefore, we used this metric for this visualization, which accounts for different rank sizes. This is earnest because this metric paints a fair picture of all the ranks through its account. This is persuasive because when we see the data with total rank size in mind, police officers actually appear to be the least problematic of all the department ranks since they have the fewest complaints per officer. We initially considered using “total complaints,” instead, but opted out since it didn’t fit our purpose of the Against proposition. The method we ended with is helpful because it allows for a fair comparison across ranks by accounting for differences in size. However, a limitation is that it is not effective when used to compare the total complaints strictly across ranks and not individuals.
  • Decision 2: Sorting the bars from lowest to highest (Score: -0.5, Slightly Deceptive)
    • The bars are intentionally arranged from lowest to highest, with the Police Office rank, our targeted point, serving as the visual baseline: the first and lowest bar. Sorting by ascending value is meant to improve readability, but here, it also shapes the perception that police officers are not the most problematic rank in the department, due to it being the lowest. This is slightly deceptive because sorting in descending order is more common, whereas we did the opposite. By reversing this, the visualization anchors Police Officers as the reference point. The alternative to this was ranking it from highest to lowest, but we felt that would not be as compelling if the police officer rank was not presented as the baseline. This works well because it immediately draws the viewer’s attention to the Police Officer rank and frames it for comparison. However, this ordering may feel unintuitive to some viewers because it starts from lowest to highest.
  • Decision 3: Adding an annotation with arrow to highlight the finding (Score: +2, Earnest)
    • Adding the annotation to the graph directs viewers to the main takeaway by summarizing the data about the police officer rank in relation to the other departments. This reduces ambiguity about the takeaway and helps the reader understand what the graph is showcasing. The arrow highlights the main focus of the graph, and the annotation emphasizes what patterns the reader should notice. When they read the annotation, it should imply that there is a large contrast between our focus point and the highest bar, which is roughly twice as large. The curved arrow creates a visual connection between the text and bar, as it directly connects that note to the specific bar that we want them to see. This approach can be seen as earnest because it directs their attention to the takeaway. An alternative choice would have been to include no annotations at all or maybe a simple label, but we chose this approach because we wanted to make the takeaway as transparent as it could be. While effective in helping viewers understand the intended takeaway, a limitation is that there may be bias in steering them toward an intended interpretation rather than allowing them to form their own conclusions.
  • Decision 4: Writing “2x difference” in the annotation rather than stating the exact numbers (Score: -0.5, Slightly Deceptive)
    • Our group phrased the annotation as “2x difference from the highest” rather than stating the exact difference of 2.07. This was done intentionally because our group felt “2x” is more memorable than the precise difference. Many people are less likely to remember the exact number, so we decided to use “2x” to help that number stick with them. This helps viewers quickly grasp that Police Officers receive complaints at half the rate of the highest rank. This worked because the annotation is short, impactful, and also easy to remember. However, what doesn’t work as well is that rounding to “2x” understates the true difference. This is slightly deceptive. It prioritizes memorability over precision, as we are sort of misleading our viewers because it isn’t an exact “2x” difference. An alternative that was considered was stating the exact numbers. We decided against this because it felt too wordy for an annotation. Even with this limitation, the annotation still directs viewers to the main takeaway without falsifying the data by that much.
  • Decision 5: Adding a note explaining how complaints per officer are calculated (Score: +1.5, Earnest)
    • Our group decided to add a note at the bottom of the visualization. This was done because we felt that viewers might not immediately understand what “complaints per officer” means or how we arrived at these numbers. Without this note, people might misinterpret how we got to these calculations or misinterpret what the y-axis is trying to represent too. This worked well because it allows viewers to know how the data was derived and calculated. This allows them to potentially see that it is a fair interpretation for the proposition. A thing that didn’t work as well is that some viewers may not read the note before they evaluate the chart because it is at the bottom of the graph. We felt that it was better to put it at the bottom because the visualization felt cleaner if we did it like that. An alternative that we considered was not to have the note at all, but we decided that transparency was more important than being as minimal as possible. The decision is earnest because it discloses how we got to those numbers instead of leaving viewers to guess how the numbers were derived.
